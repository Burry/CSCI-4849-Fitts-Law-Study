Studying the Fitts' law throughput of tap-to-click vs. press-to-click on laptop trackpads

Authors

Bryan Heiser, Grant Burry, and Vamshi Arugonda

Summary

Trackpad sensitivity and feedback has seen leaps in the last decade in terms of precision and control. But with the new transition towards thinner “ultrabook” laptops that have the power and capability of a regular laptop but without the bulk, there’s been a tendency to remove certain functions and replace them with tech that’s thinner and lighter. With trackpads, we see the loss of the separate button to click. Instead, manufacturers have cleverly made the trackpad the button resulting in two ways to select something using a trackpad. We tested the Fitts' law throughput of interaction via tapping on the trackpad to make a selection versus pressing down on the whole trackpad mechanism.

Our method of testing was brief for this experiment, but our test results came from people who had a wide demographic in tech. Grant has primarily used Apple laptops his whole life so he had a more intuitive comfort with both tapping and pressing. Bryan was a PC user for a large portion of his life until he transition to Apple. Vamshi has never owned an Apple laptop and all but one of the laptops he’s owned have had a physical button to press. There was no order to the tests we conducted as every person was instructed to do the tests on their own time. All tests were conducted using the same software (Fitts test) with a 100% error margin and 5 tests being conducted per trial, with four trials per person per interaction type (tapping versus pressing). All tests were also conducted on an Apple laptop from within the last 3 years. Our results show that pressing to click consistently yielded higher throughputs across the board, despite the background of the test participants.
